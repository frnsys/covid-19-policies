COVID-19 Policy Response

The response to COVID-19 has in many ways played out to our expectations. We are seeing the responses we’ve grown to expect in a crisis: intensification of surveillance, rolling back of protections, and financial support for irresponsible industries, to name a few. And this is amid inadequate protection of workers, monopolizing of mask equipment, anti-labor retaliation, industry opportunism, staggering unemployment, and more.

At the same time, other responses have revealed something most of us already knew (or at least suspected) – the arbitrariness of American misery. Regressive policies and enforcement procedures are being scaled back in surprising numbers. While sectors under federal control – immigration policy, higher education, healthcare, election reform – have had few COVID-related changes, those controlled by city and state governments – criminal justice, housing, transportation, utilities – have had many.

These changes were put in place to slow viral transmission and stabilize the economy, not to benefit social welfare. But what’s interesting is how much overlap there is between them. Sector by sector, the pandemic has unintentionally sparked changes pushed by reformers for decades. What couldn’t be done in years has now happened in a matter of weeks. Policy changes that before COVID were dismissed as unreasonable are now seen not only as reasonable but as necessary.

It shows us how absurd so many of our rules were to begin with. But does it also show us how easy they’ve been to change all along?

Many of these changes still don’t go far enough, come with caveats and fine print, are subject to absurd means-testing, or are only temporary. For those of us who believe that America needs serious change – and that this is the time to do it – what will it take to make many of these temporary changes permanent? These aren’t necessarily the changes we want, but they give us a glimpse of what’s possible.

Scope

What is included and not included? Our decision-making for inclusion.

  • There are many changes happening - we are trying to be comprehensive both to create a paper trail and to allow for pattern discovery
  • Some sectors have many examples: criminal justice, housing, utilities, transportation: areas under greater local and state control. Others have few changes: education, healthcare, ... those under federal control.
  • Different criteria for public vs private sector: Location. For private, national companies are listed as “nationwide”, smaller local and regional business are listed as their HQ.

Data Dictionary

Columns

  • Sector
    • Public: federal, state, and local government.
    • Private: companies
  • Level
    • Only applies to public sector entries. Provides additional information on where the change occured. Changes can be prompted by policy changes or changes in enforcement.
    • Local: city or county. Mayor, local courts, city council, also includes other actors, such as local police departments.
    • State: governor, state courts, state council.
    • Federal: executive, federal courts, congress.
  • Location
    • For local level: city and/or county and state
    • For state level: state
    • For federal level: nationwide
  • Who
    • Information on who or what instigated the change. Caveat: it is not always clear who decided on the change vs. who implemented the change. We’ve added all publicly available information.
  • Start Date
    • What day the change was officially announced.
  • End Date
    • What day the change is officially supposed to end.
    • Categories: date, unspecified, until crisis ends.
      • Unspecified: no end date explicitly given.
      • Until crisis ends: end date given for end of outbreak.
      • N/A: one-off event.

Categories and Definitions

  • CRIMINAL JUSTICE
    • Released from jail: Jails are short-term local facilities (run by city or county) for people in pre-trial detention or serving sentences less than a year.
    • Released from prison: Prisons are longer-term facilities (run by the state or federal government) for people convicted of longer sentences.
    • Released from pretrial detention: Pretrial detention is the act of detaining someone accused of a crime who has not yet had their case heard. Most of the 550,000 people in pretrial detention in 2020 were there because they couldn’t afford to post bail.
  • HOUSING
    • Expanded shelters: Expansion of shelters (such as using hotels/motels) for housing the homeless.
    • Suspended evictions: Suspension of evictions, especially residential evictions. In general this still means that rent is due, just at a later date.
    • Suspended rent: Suspension of rent; again, usually means that the rent is still owed, just at a later date.
  • IMMIGRATION
    • Reduced arrests: Reduction in arrests for immigration-related reasons.
    • Released from detention: Release of people held for immigration-related reasons from detention facilities.
  • SOCIAL SERVICES
    • Free meals: Expansion of free meal services, especially in terms of who has access.
    • Free childcare: Provision of free/public childcare services.
    • Reduced barriers: Lifting or easing of access restrictions to social services.
  • EDUCATION
    • Improved access to resources: Access to educational resources like textbooks or laptops are made free or otherwise greatly expanded.
    • Suspended loan payments, interest, or collection: Student loan payments, collection, or interest are suspended.
  • ELECTIONS
    • Eased voter registration: Automatic vote registration.
    • Eased voting: Vote-by-mail, early voting, absentee voting.
  • EMPLOYMENT
    • Expanded employment benefits: Paid sick leave, flexible hours, remote work, etc.
    • Expanded unemployment benefits: Either expansion in the benefits themselves or expansion in who qualifies (such as independent contractors, freelancers, etc).
    • Removed employment barriers: Removal or easing of restrictions on who can work or who is allowed to do certain kinds of work and where.
  • HEALTHCARE
    • Lifted regulatory barriers: Removal or easing of restrictions limiting who has access to certain medical services or how people can otherwise interact with the healthcare system.
    • Expanded benefits: Expansion of healthcare benefits.
    • Deprivatized health system: Lessening of private influence/control over the healthcare system.
    • Capped drug prices: Limitations on prices of drugs.
  • TRANSPORTATION
    • Expanded bike lanes: Expansion of bike infrastructure.
    • Pedestrianized streets: Restrictions on car access to roads to allow for pedestrian traffic.
    • Suspended fares: Suspension of fares or fare collection for public transit.
  • UTILITIES
    • Free internet: Free internet service.
    • Suspended data/speed caps: Suspension of data and/or speed caps for internet services.
    • Suspended shutoffs and late fees: Suspension of shutoffs and/or late fees for services.
    • Restored service: Restoration of already-shutoff services.
